# 🤰 Labor Prep Daily | Sunday, May 31, 2026 (PT)

**Today's theme:** Breaking the water (AROM)  
_No going back; hospital time limits; when waiting is valid_


---
### 1. After AROM, the water stays broken

Artificial rupture of membranes means the amniotic sac is opened on purpose. That change is permanent—you cannot reseal it. Labor may pick up, or it may not. Either way, your team will watch infection risk and how long membranes have been open. Ask your OB or midwife what changes for your plan once water is broken.

📚 **Source:** The Birth Partner by Penny Simkin (book)
https://www.pennysimkin.com/
**Partner move:** Before anyone breaks the water, ask quietly: once this happens, what path are we on—and can we pause to decide together?

---
### 2. Broken water can start a hospital clock

Many hospitals track hours from rupture—membranes open—to delivery or next step. Policies vary: some allow many hours if vitals are good; others move toward Pitocin or cesarean sooner. Early labor at the hospital, with little to do, often leads to unnecessary interventions. Know your facility's typical timeline before you're in the room.

📚 **Source:** Birthful by Maya Litz (podcast)
https://birthful.com/
**Partner move:** Ask the nurse or provider directly: does breaking her water start a clock for us—and what happens at each hour mark?

---
### 3. Waiting after AROM can be reasonable

If the baby looks well on monitoring and there are no signs of infection, waiting for contractions to strengthen on their own is sometimes appropriate. Use a simple decision framework: what is the problem, what are the options, what are the risks and benefits, and what if we wait? Your clinical team—not this lesson—sets safe limits.

📚 **Source:** Evidence Based Birth by Rebecca Dekker, PhD, RN (blog)
https://evidencebasedbirth.com/replay-ebb-357-making-decisions-about-elective-induction-of-labor-with-dr-ann-peralta-kari-radoff-cnm-from-partner-to-decide/
**Partner move:** If someone suggests AROM to speed things up, help her ask: what problem are we fixing, and what happens if we give it more time?

_Education only — your OB and hospital policies decide what's safe for you._
